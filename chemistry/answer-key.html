Section B — Short Response (4 marks each, attempt any 11)

B1. Empirical: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms (e.g. CH₂O for glucose). Molecular: actual ratio (e.g. C₆H₁₂O₆). Compute moles per element: C = 40/12 = 3.33, H = 6.7/1 = 6.7, O = 53.3/16 = 3.33. Divide by smallest (3.33): C:H:O = 1:2:1 → CH₂O. (2 marks definition, 2 marks calculation.)

B2. Avogadro's law: equal volumes of gases at same T and P contain equal numbers of molecules. Hence 1 mole of any gas (6.022 × 10²³ molecules) occupies the same volume at STP — experimentally 22.4 L. (2 + 2.)

B3. Solvent extraction: separating a solute from a mixture by shaking with a suitable immiscible solvent in which the solute is more soluble. Example: extracting iodine from aqueous solution into CCl₄ in a separating funnel. (2 marks principle, 2 marks example.)

B4. Diagram: filter-paper strip, baseline, sample spot, solvent reservoir, solvent front. Rf = (distance moved by solute)/(distance moved by solvent front). (2 marks diagram, 2 marks Rf.)

B5. From KMT, pressure P = (1/3) ρ c̄², where ρ depends on N/V. At constant T, c̄² is constant → P ∝ 1/V, which is Boyle's law. (2 marks KMT expression, 2 marks derivation.)

B6. Real gases have intermolecular forces (matter at high P) and finite molecular volume (matter when V is small). At high P / low T, both become significant — gas behaviour deviates. van der Waals correction: (P + an²/V²)(V − nb) = nRT. (2 marks reasoning, 2 marks equation.)

B7. H-bond between O–H of one water molecule and lone pair on O of another. Consequences: ice less dense than water (open hexagonal lattice) → ice floats; high boiling point of water vs H₂S. (2 marks H-bond, 2 marks consequence.)

B8. Ionic (e.g. NaCl, KBr): high m.p., conduct in molten/solution, brittle, soluble in water. Molecular (e.g. ice, dry ice): low m.p., poor conductor, soft, soluble in non-polar solvents. (2 marks each.)

B9. n (principal — energy level, 1, 2, 3 …); l (azimuthal — subshell shape, 0 to n−1; s, p, d, f); mₗ (magnetic — orientation, −l to +l); mₛ (spin, ±½). (1 mark each.)

B10. Heisenberg: it is impossible to determine simultaneously, with absolute precision, the position and momentum of a microscopic particle. Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π. (2 + 2.)

B11. In CH₄, one 2s and three 2p orbitals of carbon mix to form four equivalent sp³ hybrid orbitals at 109.5°, each overlapping with 1s of H to form a σ bond. Tetrahedral geometry. (2 marks process, 2 marks geometry & angle.)

B12. Hess's law: total enthalpy change of a reaction is the same whether it takes place in one step or in several steps. Useful when ΔH cannot be measured directly. Example: ΔH for C → CO₂ via CO is the sum of ΔH(C→CO) + ΔH(CO→CO₂). (2 + 2.)

B13. Kc = [NO₂]² / [N₂O₄] = (0.2)² / 0.4 = 0.04/0.4 = **0.1 M**. (2 marks expression, 2 marks numerical.)

B14. Le Chatelier: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed, the equilibrium shifts to oppose the change. For Haber N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, ΔH < 0: increase P shifts forward (fewer moles), decrease T shifts forward (exothermic). Optimal industrial conditions: ~200 atm, ~450 °C, Fe catalyst. (2 marks principle, 2 marks application.)

B15. Moles NaOH = 4/40 = 0.1. Volume = 0.25 L. M = 0.1 / 0.25 = **0.4 M**. (2 + 2.)

B16. First: mass of substance deposited at electrode is proportional to charge passed: m ∝ Q. Second: when same charge is passed through different electrolytes, masses deposited are proportional to their equivalent weights: m₁/m₂ = E₁/E₂. (2 + 2.)

B17. Order: sum of exponents of concentration terms in the experimental rate law (can be fractional, zero). Molecularity: number of reactant species in the elementary step (always whole). E.g. 2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂: order = 3 experimentally; molecularity = 3. Decomposition of N₂O₅: order = 1; molecularity = 1. (2 marks definitions, 2 marks examples.)

Section C — Long Response (8 marks each)

Q18 (main). (a) Reaction: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + CO₂ + H₂O. Moles CaCO₃ = 5/100 = 0.05. Moles HCl = 0.020 × 2 = 0.04. Required HCl for 0.05 mol CaCO₃ = 0.10 mol. Available = 0.04 mol → HCl is limiting. Moles CO₂ from 0.04 mol HCl = 0.04 / 2 = 0.02. Mass CO₂ = 0.02 × 44 = 0.88 g. (2 marks moles, 1 mark identifying LR, 2 marks CO₂ mass.) (b) Percent yield = (actual / theoretical) × 100. But theoretical here (0.88 g) < actual claimed (1.6 g) → result invalid; check for error in problem. Assuming theoretical 1.76 g (i.e. CaCO₃ limiting): yield = (1.6/1.76) × 100 = 90.9 %. (2 marks formula, 1 mark interpretation.)

Tutor note: model paper deliberately keeps the LR question simple. Examiners typically use clean numbers. Adjust for your students.

Q19 (main). (a) Bohr's postulates: electron in stationary orbit doesn't radiate; angular momentum mvr = nh/2π; energy emitted/absorbed in transitions equals hν. Equating Coulomb attraction = centripetal force: kZe²/r² = mv²/r → r = kZe²/(mv²). Combine with mvr = nh/2π → v = nh/(2πmr). Substituting: r = n²h² / (4π²mkZe²). For H (Z=1), rₙ = 0.529 n² Å. Energy: E = KE + PE = ½mv² − kZe²/r = −kZe²/(2r) → Eₙ = −2π²mk²Z²e⁴/(n²h²) = −13.6 Z²/n² eV. (3 marks radius, 3 marks energy.) (b) First Balmer line (n₁=2, n₂=3): 1/λ = R (1/4 − 1/9) = R × 5/36 → λ = 36/(5R) = 656 nm. (2 marks.)

Q20 (main). (a) Le Chatelier: if a system at equilibrium is disturbed by change in concentration, pressure, or temperature, the system adjusts to partially undo the change. (i) Ammonia synthesis: N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, 4 mol → 2 mol. Increase P → equilibrium shifts forward, more NH₃. (ii) Contact process: 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃, ΔH < 0. Decrease T → equilibrium shifts forward, more SO₃; but rate slows, so industrial compromise at 450 °C with V₂O₅ catalyst. (2 marks statement, 2 marks each application.) (b) Kp = P_SO₃² / (P_SO₂² · P_O₂). (2 marks.)

Q21 (main). (a) Daniell cell: Zn rod in ZnSO₄ (anode), Cu rod in CuSO₄ (cathode), salt bridge (KCl/agar) connecting both half-cells. Anode: Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation). Cathode: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (reduction). Net: Zn + Cu²⁺ → Zn²⁺ + Cu. Cell representation: Zn | Zn²⁺(1M) || Cu²⁺(1M) | Cu. EMF = 1.10 V. (3 marks setup with diagram, 3 marks reactions & notation.) (b) Q = It = 2 × 1800 = 3600 C. From m = MIt/(nF): m = (63.5)(3600)/(2 × 96500) = 1.184 g. (2 marks.)

Q22 (main). (a) Rate: change in conc per unit time. Rate constant k: proportionality constant in rate law, depends only on T and catalyst. Half-life t₁/₂: time for concentration to fall to half its initial value. Activation energy Eₐ: minimum energy barrier reactants must cross. (1 mark each.) (b) Arrhenius: k = A e^(−Eₐ/RT). Taking ln: ln k = ln A − Eₐ/(RT). Plot of ln k (y-axis) vs 1/T (x-axis) is linear with slope −Eₐ/R. Hence Eₐ = −R × slope. (2 marks equation, 2 marks plot & extraction of Eₐ.)
