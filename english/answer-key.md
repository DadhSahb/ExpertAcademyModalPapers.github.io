## Section F — Paragraph / Dialogue / Story (10 marks)

**Model — Paragraph on "The Importance of Time" (~110 words):**

> Time is the most precious gift bestowed upon us, yet it is the one we squander most carelessly. Unlike money, lost time can never be regained. Every great achievement in human history has been the fruit of disciplined use of time, while every regret stems from its waste. A student who utilises every hour productively excels in life; one who postpones his duties is left behind. The morning hours are particularly golden — fresh, quiet, and full of energy. We should therefore plan each day, set clear goals, and never delay what can be done now. Indeed, those who respect time find that time, in turn, respects them.

## Section G-1 — Textbook short answers (16 marks, 4 × 4)

**(i) Lesson Jesse's father taught him.**
Jesse's father taught him that hard work, perseverance, and a deep love for the soil could turn even the most barren clearing into a productive piece of land. He instilled the values of patience, faith, and the dignity of physical labour, showing that nothing worthwhile comes without effort.

**(ii) Why the Bittering family stayed on Mars.**
At first, the family wanted to return to Earth, but they were trapped on Mars by the Earth war. As the months passed, the Martian climate, food, and water gradually transformed them physically and mentally — their eyes turned golden and they began to think and behave like Martians. By the time rescue arrived, they no longer identified themselves as humans and had moved into the abandoned Martian villas.

**(iii) How Mrs Jones changed Roger's behaviour.**
When Roger tried to snatch Mrs Jones's purse, instead of handing him to the police she took him home, fed him, washed his face, and even gave him ten dollars for the blue suede shoes he wanted. Her unexpected kindness, trust, and motherly concern shamed Roger and awakened his conscience. He left her house transformed, unable even to say "thank you," yet remembering her for the rest of his life.

**(iv) Irony in *The Reward*.**
The story is built on the painful irony that Mr Jacob Salsibacca, after a lifetime of honest, devoted service to his employers, receives an embarrassingly small reward — an old, useless watch. The deeper irony is that he had counted upon a generous gift to support him in his old age, but is left with empty hands, illustrating how the modern world rewards loyalty with neglect.

**(v) What Jim and Della sacrificed.**
On Christmas Eve, Della sold her beautiful long hair to buy a platinum chain for Jim's gold watch. Jim, in turn, sold his watch to buy a set of jewelled combs for Della's hair. Each sacrifice rendered the other's gift useless, yet revealed the depth of their love — the true magi of their humble home.

**(vi) Central conflict in *Heat Lightning*.**
The play is set in a remote roadside diner during a stormy night. The conflict centres on the moral struggle of the diner's owner, Myrtle, who suspects that the well-dressed stranger seeking shelter is in fact a wanted murderer. She must decide whether to act on her suspicion or remain silent, while juggling her own fears and the safety of her young helper. The dramatic tension is heightened by the storm and the arrival of the police, building toward a climax that questions guilt, fear, and human judgment.

> Marking: 4 marks each — 2 marks accuracy of content, 2 marks language.

## Section G-2 — Poem reference (4 marks)

> *"I hear leaves drinking rain; / I hear rich leaves on top / Giving the poor beneath drop after drop."*

These lines are taken from the poem **"The Rain"** by **W. H. Davies**. The poet describes the music of falling rain. He hears the upper, "rich" leaves catching most of the rain and then dripping it onto the lower, "poor" leaves. Through this simple natural image, the poet conveys a powerful social message — that wealth and bounty are passed from the privileged to the less fortunate, and there is harmony in the natural order even between strong and weak. The contrast between "rich" and "poor" leaves makes the poem an allegory for human society. *(2 marks identification, 2 marks central idea.)*

## Section H — Essay (20 marks)

**Model essay — "Pollution and its Remedies" (~280 words):**

> Pollution has emerged as one of the gravest threats facing humanity in the twenty-first century. The very elements that sustain life — air, water, and soil — are being poisoned by human activity, endangering not only our own health but also the survival of countless other species.
>
> Air pollution is caused chiefly by smoke from vehicles, factories, and the burning of fossil fuels. The polluted air carries microscopic particles and toxic gases such as carbon monoxide and sulphur dioxide that damage our lungs and contribute to global warming. Water pollution, equally alarming, results from the discharge of industrial waste, sewage, and agricultural runoff into rivers and oceans. Marine life perishes, drinking water becomes unsafe, and waterborne diseases spread rapidly. Soil pollution from pesticides, plastic waste, and untreated solid waste degrades agricultural land and enters our food chain.
>
> The remedies, fortunately, are within our reach. Governments must enforce strict environmental laws, regulate industrial emissions, and invest in renewable energy sources such as solar and wind. Public transport must be improved so that the load of private vehicles is reduced. Citizens must play their part by planting trees, conserving water, segregating waste, and avoiding single-use plastics. Educational institutions should integrate environmental awareness into curricula so that the next generation grows up with respect for nature.
>
> The fight against pollution is not the responsibility of the government alone — it is a moral duty of every individual. If each one of us makes small but consistent efforts, we can together restore the health of our planet. Let us remember that we have not inherited the earth from our ancestors; we have only borrowed it from our children, and we must return it to them in a far better condition than we received it.

> Marking: 6 marks content & ideas, 5 marks organisation (introduction-body-conclusion), 4 marks vocabulary, 3 marks grammar, 2 marks spelling & punctuation.
